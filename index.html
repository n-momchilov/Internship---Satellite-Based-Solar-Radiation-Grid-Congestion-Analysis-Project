How satellite imagery reveals grid congestion

The problem

The Dutch electricity grid was designed for one-way power flow: from power plants to homes. Rooftop solar panels reversed this. When too many panels in one area push electricity back into the grid, cables and transformers get overloaded. This is called grid congestion. In Amsterdam, operator Liander has already declared congestion in several districts. New solar connections are being refused because the local grid cannot handle more feed-in.

Why satellite imagery?

Grid operators know where congestion happens, but their data is private and hard to access. Satellite imagery is publicly available and covers entire cities at once. The idea behind this project is: can we look at a city from space and predict where the grid is likely congested, without needing access to internal grid data?

What satellites see that matters for congestion

🏢

Building density

Sentinel-2 imagery reveals built-up surface density through the NDBI index. Denser built areas tend to have more connections and higher solar feed-in potential.

🗂️

Land use patterns

Satellite indices combined with CBS neighbourhood statistics show residential, commercial, or industrial character. Each type has a different consumption-to-generation ratio and grid load.

☀️

Solar panel penetration

Network operator data shows the share of connections with solar panels per cell. Risk is higher when many panels feed one substation instead of being spread across the city.

The analysis pipeline

Step 1

Grid congestion analysis with Sentinel-2 and Dutch public datasets. Random Forest predicts High / Medium / Low per 1x1 km cell.

Step 2

Solar radiation estimation from Meteosat SEVIRI at 15-min resolution. This captures cloud variation that 5-day Sentinel cannot.

Step 3

Validation and comparison: high radiation plus high congestion means solar-grid mismatch. Low radiation and high congestion points to load from industry or other sources.

What the map shows

Congestion uses a categorical scale: red is high risk, orange medium, green low. The radiation layer is diverging: red means more sun than city average, blue means less. Per-cell overlays show building density (yellow heat), PV penetration (red, from network data), and land use type (color by residential/commercial/industrial/mixed) to explain why some cells have high congestion risk.

Limitations

This prototype uses dummy data. A real version would train on actual satellite imagery and validate against Liander maps. The 1x1 km cell size balances detail and data needs. It does not detect individual rooftop panels, only area-level behavior.
